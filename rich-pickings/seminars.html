Keynote address

The role of the Groceries Code Adjudicator

Christine Tacon CBE – Groceries Code Adjudicator

The Groceries Code Adjudicator was established by an Act of Parliament on June 25th 2013.

The Groceries Supply Code of Practice (the Code) was established in February 2010 following two Competition Commission inquiries. The Code sets out standards of fair dealing between direct suppliers and the ten retailers with an annual UK groceries turnover >£1bn. The Code requires these retailers to have contracts with their direct suppliers and prohibits a range of practices which the Competition Commission investigated and reported as having an adverse effect on competition in the groceries sector and on consumers. The Code does not cover price negotiations but includes for example requiring retailers to pay on time and prohibiting variations to contracts without due notice and retrospective discounts.

The Adjudicator was established because some suppliers felt unable to complain directly to retailers about breaches of the Code, for fear of adverse consequences. The Adjudicator can launch investigations into suspected breaches of the Code where this is supported by evidence, protecting the anonymity of complainants. The Adjudicator has a range of enforcement measures available, including the power to fine retailers if it is found that they have breached the Code, although this will only be used in the most serious cases.

Session 1: Seminar Option 1

Making the most of 2014 for the food and drink sector

Fiona Richmond – Scotland Food & Drink

2014 is a big year for Scotland, as it plays host to the Glasgow 2014 Commonwealth Games, The 2014 Ryder Cup and the second edition of Homecoming Scotland. It’s a once-in-a-lifetime chance to promote Scotland as a Land of Food and Drink on a global stage. Find out more about the ambitions for the food & drink sector; what we are doing; how to get involved and the support available. The seminar will also touch on the wider success of the food & drink story and how industry leadership body Scotland Food & Drink can help businesses grow and flourish.

Session 1: Seminar Option 2

Red meat holds the key to Scotland's agricultural future 

Jim McLaren – Quality Meat Scotland

Jim McLaren, Chairman, Quality Meat Scotland will give a presentation on critical mass in the red meat industry and the challenge of declining stock numbers as well as Quality Meat Scotland’s role in shaping a sustainable and prospering Scottish red meat industry.

Session 2: Seminar Option 1

Shaping the environment for brands in grocery

John Noble – British Brands Group

Brands hold the potential for strong commercial and competitive success in both home and export markets. To realise success though they require an environment of vigorous but fair competition, where investment in innovation, quality and reputation is safeguarded and shoppers know, and have confidence in, what they are buying. This element of the programme will explore those areas of the trading environment where efforts need to be focused for brands to thrive.

Session 2: Seminar Option 2

The value-add for whisky production by-products

Mark Simmers – Celtic Renewables

Celtic Renewables is commercialising an innovative technology which converts low value organic residues into high value commodities including renewable energy. The potential for Scotland is very significant, and the company has plans to develop the technology globally. The starting point is the use of the two low value by-products from whisky production.

Session 3: Seminar Option 1

Greater coordination essential to harvest Scotland's full food and drink potential

Bruce Davidson - Loch Fyne Oysters

To generate sustainable growth, Scotland's food and drink brand must be supported in an integrated way by all stakeholders, ranging from small producers to Michelin-starred restaurants.

Across all the components of the food and drink industry, the message has to be consistent and constant - Scotland not only produces the best, it also delivers the best experience to customers and consumers.

All segments have a role to play in helping the sector achieve its full potential and investment is necessary. Government and quangos must take the lead.

We have to seize every opportunity at home and abroad to deliver the message; making the most of events and other initiatives.

This discussion will focus on the roles stakeholders can play in building Scotland’s long term potential in food and drink.

Session 3: Seminar Option 2

Assessing global food production - who are the major players and what future role will UK agriculture have?  

Bruce Ferguson – Openfield

This seminar will examine current supply & demand - looking at the main producing areas and the effect they have on global prices. It will then look into future demand – what are the main drivers and where are the largest increases likely to be. There will also be a discussion on what challenges / opportunities lie ahead for UK / Scottish growers.

Closing address

An appetite for growth

Alasdair Gardner – Bank of Scotland

Scotland’s food and drink industry has been one of the economy’s great success stories of recent years. Domestic sales growth and further expansion into overseas markets have cemented its position as the nation’s biggest employer. The boom in the sector looks set to continue as the latest Bank of Scotland research reveals that 5,600 new jobs are predicted for Scotland’s food and drink industry by 2018. Alasdair will put into context the results of the survey, which canvassed the views of a broad cross-section of over 100 Scottish food and drink businesses with an estimated aggregate turnover of £6 billion.
